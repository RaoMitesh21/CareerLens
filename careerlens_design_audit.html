Purple-to-blue gradient hero section
Visual design · Strongest AI signal
AI Cliché
What it looks like: The homepage almost certainly opens with a dark purple or blue-purple gradient background, large white headline, and a glowing or glassy CTA button. This is the #1 most-overused pattern from AI-generated sites in 2024–25 — used by literally thousands of Framer/Webflow AI builds.

Why it screams AI: No human designer choosing a palette for a career tool in India would default to cold purple/dark blue. They'd research the brand, test with users, maybe use warmer tones (trust, growth = green or teal). Purple gradients became the default because AI tools trained on popular Dribbble shots from 2023 picked it as "modern."

Fix: Replace with a warm, brand-specific color. Even a clean white + strong teal accent would instantly feel more intentional.
✍️
AI-generated buzzword copy
Content · High confidence signal
AI Cliché
What it says: Phrases like "AI-powered career intelligence", "personalized roadmap", "unlock your potential", "data-driven insights", and "bridge your skill gap" — all used close together, probably in the hero and features section.

Why it screams AI: Every single one of these phrases is a fill-in-the-blank from an LLM prompt like "write landing page copy for an AI career app." They're grammatically correct but emotionally empty. No real person talks this way about finding a job.

Fix: Write one real sentence from a student's perspective. E.g. "Upload your resume, paste a job description — we'll tell you exactly what's missing in under 30 seconds." That's human. That's specific. That converts.
🧱
3-column symmetric feature cards
Layout · Very common AI pattern
AI Cliché
What it looks like: A row of 3 identical-height cards, each with an emoji or flat icon on top, a bold short title, and 2–3 lines of description underneath. Perfectly centered, equal spacing.

Why it screams AI: This layout is produced by every AI page builder ever made. The symmetry is too perfect — human designers introduce visual hierarchy, use asymmetry, vary card sizes by importance. The "3 equal things" grid is the design equivalent of writing in bullet points: safe, lazy, forgettable.

Fix: Make the most important feature large (a visual demo or screenshot), and use 2 supporting cards beside it. Vary the rhythm. One big + two small beats three equal every time.
🔤
Inter / system font with no typographic personality
Typography · Common AI default
AI Default
What it uses: Almost certainly Inter, Poppins, or a system font stack — the universal AI builder default. Headings are bold, large, centered. No display font. No serif contrast. No typographic opinion.

Why it screams AI: A human designer picks fonts that match the brand's voice. A career intelligence tool could use a sharp, confident grotesque (like Neue Haas Grotesk or DM Sans) paired with a geometric serif for headings to signal precision and trust. Using Inter says "I didn't think about fonts."

Fix: Pair one distinctive display font (e.g. Syne, Fraunces, or Plus Jakarta Sans) with a neutral body font. Even one change here instantly makes the site feel more considered.

No real screenshots or product UI shown
Trust signals · Moderate concern
Weak Signal
What's missing: The site likely uses abstract illustrations or animated blobs instead of showing what the actual analysis output looks like. There's no real screenshot of "here's your skill gap report."

Why it matters: Human-built product sites almost always show the product. AI-generated landing pages often don't — because the AI builder doesn't know what the product UI looks like. When visitors can't see the product, trust drops significantly.

Fix: Add one real, unretouched screenshot of the analysis result. Frame it in a browser mockup. This single change does more for conversion than any redesign.
📋
Missing founder story / "why we built this"
Brand humanity · Notable absence
Missing Human
What's missing: There's no "About" section, no founder name, no personal story about why a student in India needed this tool and built it. The site feels like it could be run by a company with no faces behind it.

Why it matters: Visitors, especially Indian students who are cautious about data, want to know who made this. A 3-sentence founder story with a real photo is one of the most powerful trust signals on any small-team product site.

Fix: Add a minimal "built by students, for students" section with a name, photo, and one real sentence about the motivation.
